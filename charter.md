### **2\. Purpose and Mission**

**Mission Statement**  
The Governance, Risk & Regulatory Alignment Working Group advances the Agentic AI Foundation’s mission by serving as a bridge between global AI policy developments and the technical advancements in the field of agentic AI systems. 

**Why this Working Group exists**  
This Working Group was formed to address:

* The lack of cohesive regulatory guidance, standards, and policy frameworks concerning agentic AI systems;   
* The lack of policy regimes that address novel risks arising from agentic AI;  
* The need for a common understanding of existing tools and risk mitigation strategies and how they could apply to the agentic AI ecosystem;  
* Ways to identify gaps in the agentic AI best practices and standards ecosystem that inhibit interoperability, as well as the associated governance issues driven by a lack of cohesive standards

**Alignment to Foundation Goals**  
The work of this WG supports:

* Providing access to transparent and collaboratively-developed insights to the emerging agentic AI policy landscape, and ensuring such insights advance the adoption of open source AI projects.

### **3\. Scope**

**In Scope** (what the WG will do)

* Identify, track, and monitor existing, and emerging, global regulations, standards, best practices, and other AI-relevant policy frameworks that affect the regulatory treatment of agentic AI systems  
* Examine how protocols (e.g., MCP and others) are implemented at an organizational level in the context of other governance processes around Agentic AI.  
* In the future, the working group may further examine some of the following:  
  * Propose standards and/or updates to existing standards to incorporate governance into agentic flows  
  * Governance, frameworks, policies for deploying agents in organizational settings  
  * Policy recommendations to ensure continued adoption of open source agentic AI projects  
  * Reversibility characterization and undo infrastructure: formal methods, protocols, and formats for characterizing action reversibility and expanding the range of reversible agentic actions  
  * AI Agent Bill of Materials standard(s), covering foundation models, scaffolding, tools, APIs, and third-party components with provenance and versioning  
  * Risk assessment and classification for agent deployments  
  * Human-in-the-Loop patterns and autonomy definitions  
  * Policies affecting Incident Reporting  
  * Policies affecting agent discovery  
  * Open source frameworks (specific to agentic AI)  
  * Evaluating and adopting/embedding specifications for specific end user verticals (e.g., FINOS AI Governance Framework)   
  * Regulatory alignment and misalignment

**Out of Scope** (what the WG will not do)

* The working group will not   
  * Provide legal advice or compliance solutions;   
  * Endorse specific commercial solutions;  
  * Exclude considerations of overlap with other working groups; or  
  * Provide legal commentary on the distribution of liability as it relates to the development or use of AI products or services.

**Assumptions and Dependencies**

* **Assumptions:** Given the rapid pace of change in this technological ecosystem, we recognize that these deliverables will likely need to (a) be updated at some regular interval, and (b) cannot be so specific and prescriptive as they discount the need for flexibility.   
* **Dependencies:** Given the expertise present in other AAIF working groups and the interdependent workstreams, ensuring open lines of communication and insight into one another’s work will be key.

### **4\. Goals, Deliverables, and Success Criteria**

**3-6 Month Goals** 

The working group’s initial goal for 2026 will focus on producing three report-style deliverables examining, analyzing, and distilling the existing landscape of governance frameworks, best practices, standards, and other policy guidance documents addressing agentic AI deployment.

**Planned Deliverables**  
For each deliverable, define owner, format, and target date.

1. **3-Month Goal:** Landscape assessment / mapping of existing agentic AI policy frameworks, guidance documents, standards, and best practices (to better understand the problems). Examples of features this assessment may examine include:  
   * Assessing how existing incident reporting regimes may apply to agentic AI deployments;  
   * Mapping areas of regulatory alignment — and misalignment — across jurisdictions, affecting agent deployment or cross-border orchestration; and  
   * Examining how open-source licensing and policy environments may influence the development and adoption of open agent frameworks.

2. **3-6 Month Goal:** Gap analysis (to articulate where the gaps are in the regulatory environment). Such analysis may include examining:  
   * The extent to which policy guidance on secure enterprise deployment of autonomous or semi-autonomous agents exists;  
   * The existence, or lack thereof, of standardized approaches for agent-level threat modeling and system security; and  
   * The prevalence, or completeness, of frameworks for agent-specific incident reporting and post-deployment accountability.  
       
3. **6-Month Goal:** Drafting and distillation of potential recommendations for how organizations can better mitigate agentic AI policy-related risks. Such recommendations may potentially include assessments of the viability of using:  
   * Standardized risk classification schemas;  
   * Guidance for organizational governance of deployed agents (e.g., approval, monitoring, and override pathways); and  
   * AI Agent Bill(s) of Materials for securing supply chain inconsistencies.

**Definition of Done (DoD)**  
A deliverable is considered complete when:

* The working group has reviewed and approved, through a general membership consensus, the final version of a given deliverable.

**Success Metrics (KPIs)** (pick a small set)
